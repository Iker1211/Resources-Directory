# The March of Thornwick — A Medieval Kingdom Description

## Overview

The March of Thornwick is a small feudal lordship in a temperate river valley, home to approximately 1,550 souls distributed across five settlements. It has existed in its present form for roughly 180 years — since the first lord, Sir Aldred the Forester, received the march from the king as a reward for service in the northern wars. The kingdom follows a manorial system where all land ultimately belongs to the lord, who grants usage rights to free tenants and assigns labour obligations to serfs. The economy is overwhelmingly agrarian but includes enough craft specialization to maintain its own tools, buildings, clothing, and a modest trade surplus in salt and wool.

## Geography

The kingdom is defined by six distinct terrain zones, each constraining and enabling different forms of human activity.

### The Thorn River

The Thorn is a perennial river, roughly forty feet wide at its narrowest and navigable by small boats for its entire course through the march. It rises in the Grey Hills to the northeast and flows south through the valley, through the marsh, and into the sea at Saltwick Cove. The river provides drinking water and irrigation for the valley fields, powers two watermills, and serves as the primary transport corridor. Fish — chiefly pike, perch, and eel — are taken year-round. The river never freezes solid, a critical fact that keeps the mills operational through winter.

### The Valley Floor

A mile-wide strip of deep alluvial loam on both sides of the Thorn, the Valley Floor is the kingdom's breadbasket and demographic core. Every arable acre is under a two-field rotation: wheat or barley one year, fallow the next, with sheep folded on the fallow to manure it. The soil is deep, well-drained, and yields roughly three grains harvested for every one sown — modest by modern standards but sufficient for survival and surplus. Approximately sixty percent of the population lives here, most in scattered farmsteads and the central village of Thornwick. Open-field strips radiate from the village, intermixing demesne land (the lord's own) with tenant strips.

### The Westwood

West of the valley, the ground rises into a managed broadleaf forest of oak, beech, ash, and hazel covering perhaps three square miles. This is not a wilderness but a carefully regulated resource. The lord's forester marks trees for felling, enforces coppice rotation (hazel and ash cut on a seven-year cycle for underwood), and collects fines for unauthorised timber-taking. The Westwood supplies all structural timber for buildings and ship repair, firewood for every hearth, charcoal for the smelter and smithy, and autumn pannage for the lord's swine herd. A swineherd drives the pigs into the forest each September to fatten on acorns and beechmast before the Martinmas slaughter.

### The Grey Hills

To the east, a low ridge of limestone and ironstone rises perhaps three hundred feet above the valley. The slopes are too thin-soiled and steep for ploughing; they support only sheep pasture. The western escarpment, however, exposes two valuable resources: iron ore at the northern end, where the smelter hamlet of Hillend clusters around the bloomery hearth, and building stone along the entire face. The quarry at Hillend produces limestone ashlar for the chapel, the great hall's foundations, and millstone grit. The sheep flocks — each marked with the lord's brand — provide wool for clothing and trade, and their carcasses supply mutton for the lord's table.

### The Southmarsh

Where the Thorn approaches the sea, the valley flattens and the river spreads into reed beds, peat bogs, and shallow meres covering roughly a square mile. The marsh is a crucial complement to the Westwood: its peat provides an alternative fuel source when timber is scarce or reserved for construction, and its reeds are harvested annually for thatching. Waterfowl — ducks, geese, and herons — are taken in quantity, and the marsh's isolation from the main settlement makes it a natural defensive barrier against coastal raiders. The hamlet of Southmere, a cluster of a dozen families, works the marsh year-round.

### Saltwick Cove

At the marsh's southern edge, a sheltered shingle beach forms the kingdom's only access to the sea. The cove is too exposed for a true harbour, but a timber jetty extends into the deeper water, sufficient for the small fishing boats and the occasional coasting cog that calls two or three times a year. The beach's most valuable asset is seawater: boiled or sun-evaporated in shallow lead-lined pans, it yields the salt that preserves the kingdom's fish and meat through winter and forms its most reliable trade export. The fishing boats — six clinker-built craft, each crewed by two or three men — work the inshore waters year-round, bringing in cod, herring, and flatfish.

## Settlements

### Thornwick (approx. 900 residents)

The central village, seat of the lord's hall, and the only settlement that can meaningfully be called a town. It sits on the west bank of the Thorn at the valley's widest point, where the river is fordable and a timber bridge now stands. The village clusters around a triangular green: the Great Hall dominates the north end, the chapel stands at the east, and the market cross occupies the centre. The watermill, brewery, smithy, tannery, and kiln are all within a few minutes' walk. Most peasants live in one-room cruck-framed cottages with clay walls and thatched roofs, though the better-off free tenants have two-room houses with plank floors and glazed windows. The population includes perhaps 180 households — counting families, servants, and dependents.

### Millthorpe (approx. 200 residents)

A linear hamlet a mile and a half upstream from Thornwick, where the river gradient steepens enough to power a second watermill. This mill is a sawmill, not a grain mill: its vertical reciprocating blade cuts oak and ash trunks into planks, beams, and joinery stock. The sawyer and his family live beside the mill; a few woodcutters' families occupy huts at the forest edge. Millthorpe exists because the Westwood's timber cannot be processed efficiently at Thornwick — hauling logs three miles is preferable to hauling green lumber back up the valley.

### Hillend (approx. 150 residents)

A mining and smelting hamlet on the western slope of the Grey Hills, where the iron ore outcrops. The settlement consists of the smelter master's house, the bloomery hearth, the quarry face, and a dozen cottages for the miners and the quarryman. Hillend is rough and isolated; the smelter master answers directly to the lord, and disputes are usually settled on the spot rather than taken to the manorial court. The smoke from the bloomery hearth is visible from Thornwick on still days.

### Southmere (approx. 125 residents)

A marsh-edge hamlet of peat-cutters and reed-harvesters, accessible from Thornwick by a raised causeway that floods in spring. The inhabitants — rough, insular, and famously independent — cut peat in blocks, dry it on the common, and transport it upriver by punt. The reeve visits quarterly to collect the lord's share; otherwise, the Southmerians manage their own affairs. They supplement their diet with wildfowl, fish, and the occasional poached swan.

### Coastwatch (approx. 175 residents)

A fishing and salt-making hamlet clustered around the jetty at Saltwick Cove. The salt pans occupy the flattest ground above the high-tide line; the fish-drying racks and smokehouse line the shore. The dockmaster lives in the largest house, a two-storey timber structure that doubles as a warehouse. Coastwatch sees more outsiders than any other settlement: the coasting trade brings merchants from other ports, and the dockmaster records every transaction for the lord's tax. A small watchtower — a fieldstone round tower, perhaps fifteen feet tall — stands on the headland north of the cove, manned at night during the summer raiding season.

## Economy

### Agriculture

The kingdom's economic foundation is grain grown on the Valley Floor. Each year, roughly 500 acres are planted — half wheat, half barley — yielding perhaps 6,000 bushels in an average year. Of this, around 1,500 bushels go to seed for the next sowing, 2,000 bushels feed the population as bread and porridge, 1,000 bushels go to ale production, and 1,500 bushels represent the surplus that feeds the lord's household, supports the craftsmen, and provides a reserve against famine. The Margrave's granary holds a standing reserve of 1.5 times annual consumption — enough to survive a complete crop failure.

Livestock includes perhaps 200 head of cattle (draught oxen and a few milk cows), 300 sheep on the Grey Hills, 200 swine in the Westwood, and horses only for the lord and his retinue. Poultry — chickens, geese, and ducks — are kept by individual households.

### Craft and Industry

The kingdom is largely self-sufficient in manufactured goods. The watermill grinds all grain into flour; the brewery produces ale for daily consumption; the smithy makes and repairs all iron tools; the tannery processes hides into leather for shoes, harnesses, and belts; the kiln fires pottery and roof tiles; the quarry supplies building stone; the sawmill converts timber into lumber. The only significant imports are wine, candle wax, lead (for the salt pans), and high-quality steel for tools that the local bloomery cannot produce. These are paid for with salt and wool exports.

### Trade

The salt pans at Coastwatch produce roughly 5,000 pounds of salt annually. About half is consumed within the march for food preservation; the rest is traded. A single coasting voyage — perhaps once a quarter — takes salt and wool down the coast to the nearest market town and returns with wine, wax, lead, and occasional luxuries like dyed cloth or a new ploughshare. The trade is small in volume but critical for the few goods the kingdom cannot produce itself.

## Governance

The lord — currently Sir Rodric, grandson of Sir Aldred — holds full manorial jurisdiction. He adjudicates disputes, collects taxes in grain and labour, commands the retinue of perhaps a dozen men-at-arms, and holds the power of life and death over his serfs. In practice, he relies heavily on his reeve, a literate serf who manages the demesne accounts and directs field labour.

The church, in the person of Father Osric, provides the only institutional counterweight to the lord's authority. The chapel collects tithes (ten percent of all grain), maintains the parish register (the only written records), and offers sanctuary to fugitives. Father Osric answers to the bishop of the diocese, not to Sir Rodric, which gives him a degree of independence rare in secular affairs.

Justice is administered at the twice-weekly manorial court, held in the Great Hall. Minor disputes — trespass, debt, stolen firewood — are settled by the lord or his reeve; serious crimes — murder, arson, rebellion — are reserved for the lord's judgement. Punishments range from fines and stocks to execution by hanging.

## Sustainability

The March of Thornwick is designed to be self-sustaining for centuries. The ecological constraints are well-understood by its inhabitants: the Westwood is coppiced on rotation, never clear-felled; the valley fields are fallowed every second year; the iron ore is extracted at a rate that does not exhaust the outcrop within living memory; the peat beds regenerate on a human timescale. The population is limited by the carrying capacity of the land — in bad years, famine and disease take the surplus; in good years, the granary fills and the surplus children either inherit or leave for the towns.

The system has survived crop failures, plague (the Black Death of 1348-49 killed perhaps a third of the population, but the survivors prospered on the newly abundant land), and occasional raiding from across the sea. It will continue to survive as long as the Thorn flows, the Westwood grows, and the Grey Hills yield their iron and stone.
